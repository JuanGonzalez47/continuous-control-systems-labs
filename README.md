## 📖 About the Course

**Continuous Control Systems** is a core course in Electronic Engineering
that introduces the mathematical and practical tools needed to analyze,
model, and design feedback control systems.

The course covers the study of dynamic systems described by differential
equations and their representations in the frequency domain. Starting from
experimental data, we learn how to obtain mathematical models of physical
systems — hydraulic, thermal, mechanical, electrical — and use those models
to design controllers that make the system behave as desired.

Throughout the course we work with two fundamental system representations:

- **Transfer Functions (TF):** a frequency-domain description of the
  input-output relationship of a system
- **State Space (SS):** a time-domain description using matrices that
  captures the internal dynamics of the system

We also study how to characterize the temporal response of a system —
settling time, percent overshoot, steady-state value — and how to design
controllers that improve that response, both in open-loop and closed-loop
configurations.

## 🏛️ A Brief Context

Control systems theory has its roots in the early 20th century, with
foundations laid by pioneers like **James Clerk Maxwell**, who first
analyzed governor stability mathematically, and **Harold Black**, who
invented the feedback amplifier in 1927. The field was further formalized
during World War II, when the need for precise control of weapons and
aircraft accelerated the development of control theory.

By the 1950s and 60s, engineers like **Walter Evans** (root locus),
**Hendrik Bode** (frequency response), and **Rudolf Kalman** (state space
and optimal control) had built the mathematical framework that we still
use today. What we study in this course is directly built on their work.

Today, control systems are everywhere: autopilots, industrial robots,
temperature regulators, power grids, medical devices, and even the
algorithms that stabilize a drone in mid-air.
